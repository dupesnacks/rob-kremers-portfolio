My Background (The Throughline)

I started my career in public relations at We Communications, managing the Microsoft account. This taught me how to position complex products for different audiences and communicate under pressure. It was foundational.

Then I moved into brand and retail strategy in the footwear and apparel industry — the most unforgiving testing ground for brand strategy. At these companies, you learn quickly that brand only matters if it drives sell-through. Every framework has to work across global markets, wholesale partners, and diverse customer segments.

At adidas, I started by joining the Amazon account team shortly after the official partnership agreement was signed. My job was to establish how adidas's go-to-market process aligned with Amazon's processes, and build the initial marketing and content strategies for the channel. This was my first lesson in translating brand strategy into channel execution.

Later at adidas, I became Senior Retail Brand Manager for global basketball. This gave me scope: managing basketball brand strategy across regions, retail partnerships, and customer segments. I also managed the personal brand and business of Damien Lillard — balancing athlete positioning with corporate sponsorship strategy. It taught me how to scale brand thinking without losing the human element.

At Nike, I built the global basketball retail brand playbook and developed retail brand strategies deployed worldwide, with particular emphasis on China market execution. I learned that brand systems at global scale require clarity, structure, and ruthless consistency — otherwise they break under complexity.

Then came a detour: My wife and I built and scaled Ace, an e-commerce barware brand, from nothing to over 70,000 units sold. We managed every single aspect — product, marketing, fulfillment, customer service, unit economics. This was essential. I needed to understand what it means to own a business end-to-end, not just the brand/marketing piece. It resets how you think about priorities.

Most recently, I've been at TuSimple and CreateAI. I started as Brand Manager and evolved to Head of Marketing, reporting directly to the CEO. I led go-to-market planning for potential new business lines, building brand identity and full GTM strategies from scratch. But the core of my work was at CreateAI, when the company was operating as an autonomous semi truck company. I managed all external-facing marketing and communications, working cross-functionally with senior technical and product leaders to distill genuinely complex autonomous driving technology into clear, compelling narratives for different audiences: regulators, fleet operators, technology partners. When your product is cutting-edge and genuinely novel, clarity becomes your competitive advantage. I learned that the gap between "what the tech does" and "why it matters" is where most communication fails.

How I Think About Work

Start With Strategy, Not Tactics

Every project begins with clarity: What's the actual problem? What are we trying to achieve? What's the market opportunity? Only after those questions are answered do we move to execution. Too many marketing teams skip this step.

Build Systems, Not Campaigns

I think in frameworks. A good system works across channels, teams, and geographies. It's repeatable. It scales. A campaign is a moment; a system is a permanent capability. The goal is always to build the latter.

Connect Marketing to Revenue

Marketing's job isn't to look good—it's to drive business results. This means thinking about sell-through, adoption, channel health, and measurable outcomes. It means understanding how sales and operations actually work.

Lead Across Silos

The best marketing decisions require input from product, sales, operations, and customer teams. But someone has to connect those dots. That's often me. I'm comfortable being the person who says "here's what needs to happen" and then leads the organization to make it real.

Clarity Over Complexity

If you can't explain something simply, you don't understand it well enough. Whether it's a brand system, a product story, or a go-to-market plan, my job is to take complexity and make it clear, compelling, and actionable.

Measure What Matters

Data without context is noise. I focus on metrics that actually matter: sell-through, adoption, revenue impact, customer acquisition cost. Numbers should tell a story and inform decisions.
